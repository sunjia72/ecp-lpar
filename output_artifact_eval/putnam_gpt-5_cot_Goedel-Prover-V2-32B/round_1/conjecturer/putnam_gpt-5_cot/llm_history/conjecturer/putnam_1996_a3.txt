Interpret the condition as a \(20\times 6\) binary matrix: rows are students, columns are courses, and entry \(1\) means the student chose that course.

The statement asks whether **for every** such matrix, there must exist:
- 5 rows (students), and
- 2 columns (courses)

so that on those 5 students, those 2 courses are either both chosen by all 5, or both rejected by all 5.

This is **false**.

A counterexample is to let the 20 students correspond exactly to the \( \binom{6}{3} = 20 \) subsets of size 3 of the 6 courses; i.e. each student chooses a different 3-course subset.

Fix any pair of courses \(\{a,b\}\).
- Students who chose both \(a,b\): choose the third course from the remaining 4, so there are \(4\).
- Students who chose neither \(a,b\): choose all 3 courses from the other 4, so there are \(\binom{4}{3}=4\).

So for every pair of courses, there are only 4 students choosing both and only 4 choosing neither. Hence no pair of courses has 5 students all in the same “both yes” or “both no” pattern.
