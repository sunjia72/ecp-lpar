Let \(R_k=\frac{10^k-1}{9}\), the base-10 repunits.  
The condition says exactly that for every \(k\ge 1\), \(f(R_k)\) is again some repunit.

A standard trick is to set
\[
g(y)=9f\!\left(\frac{y-1}{9}\right)+1.
\]
Then for every \(k\ge 1\),
\[
g(10^k)=10^{m_k}
\]
for some integer \(m_k\). So a polynomial \(g\) sends infinitely many powers of \(10\) to powers of \(10\). The only way this can happen is if \(g\) is a single monomial
\[
g(y)=10^a y^m
\]
with \(a,m\in\mathbb N\). Translating back,
\[
9f(x)+1=10^a(9x+1)^m,
\]
so
\[
f(x)=\frac{10^a(9x+1)^m-1}{9}.
\]

This includes:
- constants \( \frac{10^a-1}{9}\) (take \(m=0\)),
- the identity \(x\) (take \(a=0,m=1\)),
- \(10x+1\) (take \(a=1,m=1\)),
etc.

We must exclude \(a=m=0\), since that gives the zero polynomial, which does **not** map repunits to repunits.
