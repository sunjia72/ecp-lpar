Let  
\[
P_n(x)=\sum_{i=0}^n a_i x^i.
\]

We construct \(a_i\neq 0\) inductively.

- Start with \(a_0=a_1=1\). Then \(P_1(x)=1+x\) has exactly one real root.
- Assume \(P_n\) has \(n\) distinct real roots \(r_1<\cdots<r_n\). Since \(a_0\neq 0\), none of them is \(0\).
- Choose disjoint small intervals around each \(r_i\) on which \(P_n\) changes sign. If \(\varepsilon\) is sufficiently small, then
  \[
  Q(x)=P_n(x)+\varepsilon x^{n+1}
  \]
  has the same endpoint signs on those intervals, so by IVT it still has one real root in each interval: thus \(n\) distinct real roots remain.
- Now choose the sign of \(\varepsilon\) opposite to the leading coefficient of \(P_n\). For large positive \(x\), \(Q(x)\sim \varepsilon x^{n+1}\), so its sign at \(+\infty\) is opposite to its sign at some large \(M\) where \(P_n\) still dominates. Hence there is one additional real root \(>M\).

So \(Q\) has \(n+1\) distinct real roots. Setting \(a_{n+1}=\varepsilon\neq 0\) completes the induction.
