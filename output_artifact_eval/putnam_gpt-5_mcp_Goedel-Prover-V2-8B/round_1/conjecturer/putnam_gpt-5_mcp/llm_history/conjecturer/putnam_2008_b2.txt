Let
\[
F_0(x)=\log x,\qquad 
F_{n+1}(x)=\int_0^x F_n(t)\,dt \quad (x>0).
\]

We claim that for every \(n\ge 0\) and \(x>0\),
\[
F_n(x)=\frac{x^n}{n!}\bigl(\log x-H_n\bigr),
\]
where
\[
H_n=\sum_{k=1}^n \frac1k
\]
is the \(n\)-th harmonic number (with \(H_0=0\)).

This is true for \(n=0\).  
If it holds for \(n\), then
\[
F_{n+1}(x)=\int_0^x \frac{t^n}{n!}(\log t-H_n)\,dt.
\]
Using
\[
\int t^n\log t\,dt=\frac{t^{n+1}}{n+1}\log t-\frac{t^{n+1}}{(n+1)^2},
\]
we get
\[
F_{n+1}(x)=\frac{x^{n+1}}{(n+1)!}\left(\log x-\Bigl(H_n+\frac1{n+1}\Bigr)\right)
=\frac{x^{n+1}}{(n+1)!}(\log x-H_{n+1}).
\]
So the formula is proved.

Now set \(x=1\). Since \(\log 1=0\),
\[
F_n(1)= -\frac{H_n}{n!}.
\]
Hence
\[
\frac{n!\,F_n(1)}{\log n}=-\frac{H_n}{\log n}.
\]
But the standard asymptotic is
\[
H_n=\log n+\gamma+o(1),
\]
so
\[
\frac{H_n}{\log n}\to 1.
\]
Therefore
\[
\frac{n!\,F_n(1)}{\log n}\to -1.
\]
