Let  
\[
A=P+iQ\in \mathbb C[X].
\]
Then
\[
A\overline A=(P+iQ)(P-iQ)=P^2+Q^2=X^{2n}+1.
\]

Now \(X^{2n}+1\) has exactly \(2n\) simple complex roots, namely the \(2n\)-th roots of \(-1\), and they come in \(n\) conjugate pairs \(\{\alpha,\bar\alpha\}\).

Because the roots are simple, \(A\) and \(\overline A\) cannot share a root. So for each conjugate pair \(\{\alpha,\bar\alpha\}\), exactly one root goes into \(A\) and the other goes into \(\overline A\). Thus there are
\[
2^n
\]
ways to choose the root set of \(A\).

So
\[
A=c\prod_{j=1}^n (X-r_j),
\]
where for each conjugate pair we choose one \(r_j\), and \(c\in \mathbb C\) satisfies \(|c|=1\) since
\[
A\overline A = X^{2n}+1
\]
has leading coefficient \(1\).

Now the leading coefficients of \(P\) and \(Q\) are respectively
\[
\Re(c),\qquad \Im(c),
\]
because
\[
P=\frac{A+\overline A}{2},\qquad Q=\frac{A-\overline A}{2i}.
\]
For the condition \(\deg P>\deg Q\), since both \(A\) and \(\overline A\) have degree \(n\), we must have \(\Im(c)=0\), otherwise \(Q\) would also have degree \(n\). Hence \(c=\pm 1\). For each root choice, that gives exactly \(2\) possibilities.

Therefore the total number of pairs \((P,Q)\) is
\[
2\cdot 2^n=2^{n+1}.
\]

Python check for small \(n\) gave:
- \(n=1\): \(4\)
- \(n=2\): \(8\)
- \(n=3\): \(16\)
- etc., matching \(2^{n+1}\).
