### Lower bound
Take
\[
P_n(x)=n-\frac1n(x+x^2+\cdots+x^{n-1})+x^n.
\]
Then in \(P_n(x)^2\),

- for \(1\le k\le n-1\),
  \[
  c_k=-2+\frac{k-1}{n^2}<0,
  \]
- for \(k=n\),
  \[
  c_n=2n+\frac{n-1}{n^2}>0,
  \]
- for \(n+1\le k\le 2n-1\),
  \[
  c_k=-\frac{2}{n}+\frac{2n-k-1}{n^2}=-\frac{k+1}{n^2}<0.
  \]

Also \(c_0=n^2>0\) and \(c_{2n}=1>0\).  
So exactly the \(2n-2\) coefficients other than \(c_0,c_n,c_{2n}\) are negative.

Hence the supremum is at least \(2n-2\).

### Upper bound
Assume, for contradiction, that all interior coefficients \(c_1,\dots,c_{2n-1}\) are negative.

After replacing \(P\) by \(-P\) if needed, assume \(a_0>0\).

Now induct on \(k=1,\dots,n-1\). Since
\[
c_k=2a_0a_k+\sum_{i=1}^{k-1} a_i a_{k-i},
\]
and \(c_k<0\), starting from \(c_1=2a_0a_1<0\), we get successively
\[
a_1<0,\ a_2<0,\ \dots,\ a_{n-1}<0.
\]

Apply the same argument to the reversed polynomial
\[
x^nP(1/x)=a_n+a_{n-1}x+\cdots+a_0x^n.
\]
Its square has the same interior coefficients, just reversed, so the same reasoning shows that \(a_0,\dots,a_{n-1}\) all have sign opposite to \(a_n\). Since \(a_0>0\), this forces \(a_n>0\).

Then
\[
c_n=2a_0a_n+\sum_{i=1}^{n-1} a_i a_{n-i}>0,
\]
because \(a_0,a_n>0\) and each \(a_i,a_{n-i}<0\) for \(1\le i\le n-1\). This contradicts \(c_n<0\).

So not all interior coefficients can be negative. Since \(c_0>0\) and \(c_{2n}>0\) always, there are at least three nonnegative coefficients, hence at most
\[
(2n+1)-3=2n-2

negative coefficients.
