Let the roots be \(\alpha,\beta\in\mathbb C\). Then
\[
x^2+bx+c=(x-\alpha)(x-\beta),\qquad \alpha+\beta=-b,\ \alpha\beta=c.
\]

We want both roots to satisfy \(|\alpha|,|\beta|<1\).

Key identities:
\[
1+b+c=(1-\alpha)(1-\beta),\qquad
1-b+c=(1+\alpha)(1+\beta),\qquad
1-c=1-\alpha\beta.
\]

### Necessity
If both roots are in the open unit disk, then:

- \(1-c>0\):  
  if roots are nonreal, they are conjugates so \(c=\alpha\beta=|\alpha|^2<1\);  
  if roots are real, then \(\alpha,\beta\in(-1,1)\), so \(\alpha\beta<1\).

- \(1+b+c>0\):  
  if nonreal, \((1-\alpha)(1-\beta)=|1-\alpha|^2>0\);  
  if real, both \(1-\alpha,1-\beta>0\).

- \(1-b+c>0\): similarly, \((1+\alpha)(1+\beta)>0\).

So the conditions are necessary:
\[
1+b+c>0,\qquad 1-b+c>0,\qquad 1-c>0.
\]

### Sufficiency
Now assume
\[
1+b+c>0,\qquad 1-b+c>0,\qquad 1-c>0.
\]

- If the roots are nonreal, then they are conjugates, so \(c=\alpha\beta=|\alpha|^2\). Since \(1-c>0\), we get \(|\alpha|<1\), hence both roots have modulus \(<1\).

- If the roots are real, let them be \(\alpha\le \beta\).  
  Since
  \[
  f(1)=1+b+c>0,\qquad f(-1)=1-b+c>0,
  \]
  and \(f(x)=(x-\alpha)(x-\beta)\) is negative exactly on \((\alpha,\beta)\), the interval \([\alpha,\beta]\) cannot contain either \(-1\) or \(1\). Thus either both roots lie in \((-1,1)\), or both are \(\ge 1\), or both are \(\le -1\).  
  But \(1-c>0\) means \(\alpha\beta<1\), ruling out the last two possibilities. Hence \(\alpha,\beta\in(-1,1)\).

Therefore the desired condition is exactly
\[
1+b+c>0,\quad 1-b+c>0,\quad 1-c>0.
\]
