A supporting Python check on many small reduced rationals shows `height(T(x)) > height(x)` for  
\(T(x)=x-\frac1x\), suggesting an infinite backward chain is impossible.

Informal proof: write any \(x\in S\) in lowest terms as \(x=a/b\) with \(b>0\), \(\gcd(|a|,b)=1\), and \(a\neq 0,\pm b\). Then
\[
T(x)=\frac{a^2-b^2}{ab}.
\]
Since \(\gcd(|a|b,\ |a^2-b^2|)=1\), in lowest terms the denominator is \(|a|b\) and the numerator has absolute value \(|a^2-b^2|\).

Define the “height”
\[
h\!\left(\frac{m}{n}\right)=|m|+n
\]
for a reduced rational with \(n>0\). Then
\[
h(T(x))=|a^2-b^2|+|a|b.
\]
Let \(u=\max(|a|,b)\), \(v=\min(|a|,b)\). Since \(u\ge v+1\),
\[
h(T(x))-h(x)=u^2-v^2+uv-u-v>0
\]
(the minimum occurs at \(u=v+1\), where it equals \(v(v+1)>0\)). Thus \(h(T(x))>h(x)\).

So if \(y=T(x)\), every preimage \(x\) has strictly smaller height than \(y\). Hence no rational can have arbitrarily long backward orbits under \(T\): a chain
\[
x_n \mapsto x_{n-1} \mapsto \cdots \mapsto x_0=y
\]
would force
\[
h(x_n)<h(x_{n-1})<\cdots<h(x_0),
\]
so its length is bounded by \(h(y)-1\). Therefore
\[
\bigcap_{n\ge 1} f^{[n]}(\mathrm{univ})=\varnothing.
\]

So the proposition is **True**.
