\]
Then the first constant is \(\inf r(x)\) and the second is \(\sup r(x)\).

  \[
  \sin x \le \frac{2}{\pi}x.
  \]
  Also \(x\le \pi/2\) implies
  \[
  \frac{2}{\pi}x \le \frac{4}{\pi^2}x(\pi-x).
  \]
  By symmetry, this holds on all of \([0,\pi]\). Equality occurs at \(x=\pi/2\), so
  \[
  \sup r(x)=\frac{4}{\pi^2}.
  \]

- **Lower bound**: define
  \[
  h(x)=\sin x-\frac{x(\pi-x)}{\pi}.
  \]
  On \([0,\pi/2]\),
  \[
  h'(x)=\cos x-1+\frac{2x}{\pi},\qquad
  h''(x)=\frac{2}{\pi}-\sin x.
  \]
  Since \(\sin x\) increases on \([0,\pi/2]\), \(h''\) is first positive then negative, so \(h'\) first increases then decreases. But
  \[
  h'(0)=0,\qquad h'(\pi/2)=0,

  \[
  \sin x\ge \frac{x(\pi-x)}{\pi}.
  \]

  \[
  \inf r(x)=\frac1\pi.
  \]
