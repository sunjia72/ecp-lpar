Let \(S(N)\) be the number of squarish integers in \(\{1,\dots,N\}\).

If \(k^2<n<(k+1)^2\), then the nearest square to \(n\) is either \(k^2\) or \((k+1)^2\), and since
\[
(k+1)^2-k^2=2k+1
\]
is odd, there is no tie for an integer \(n\). Thus \(n\) is squarish exactly when its distance to the nearer endpoint is a square. For each square \(j^2\le k\), this gives the two numbers
\[
k^2+j^2,\qquad (k+1)^2-j^2.
\]
So the open interval \((k^2,(k+1)^2)\) contains exactly \(2\lfloor \sqrt{k}\rfloor\) squarish integers.

Hence for \(m^2\le N<(m+1)^2\),
\[
S(N)=m+2\sum_{k=1}^{m-1}\lfloor \sqrt{k}\rfloor+O(\sqrt m).
\]

\[
\sum_{k=1}^{m}\lfloor \sqrt{k}\rfloor

\]
so
\[
S(N)=\frac43 m^{3/2}+O(m)=\frac43 N^{3/4}+o(N^{3/4}).
\]

\[
\frac{S(N)}{N^\alpha}\to \beta
