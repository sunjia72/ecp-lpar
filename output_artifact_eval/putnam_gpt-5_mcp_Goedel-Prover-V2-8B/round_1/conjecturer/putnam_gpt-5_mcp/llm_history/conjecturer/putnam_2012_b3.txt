Let \(W_d\) be the set of winners on day \(d\).  
We want one distinct winning team from each of the \(2n-1\) days, i.e. a system of distinct representatives for the family \(\{W_d\}\).

By Hall’s theorem, it is enough to show:

\[
\left|\bigcup_{d\in S} W_d\right| \ge |S|
\]

for every set \(S\) of days.

So fix \(S\), with \(|S|=s\), and let

\[
U=\bigcup_{d\in S} W_d,\qquad |U|=r.
\]

Every team outside \(U\) lost on every day in \(S\). Hence on each day in \(S\), each team outside \(U\) was matched against a team in \(U\).

Now the schedule is a round-robin: each pair of teams plays at least once, and since each team has exactly \(2n-1\) days and \(2n-1\) possible opponents, each pair actually plays **exactly once**.

Therefore, for any fixed team \(x\notin U\), its opponents on the \(s\) days of \(S\) are \(s\) distinct teams, all lying in \(U\). So \(U\) must contain at least \(s\) teams:

\[
r \ge s.
\]

Thus Hall’s condition holds for every \(S\), so there exists an injective choice of a winner, one from each day.

So the statement is always true.
