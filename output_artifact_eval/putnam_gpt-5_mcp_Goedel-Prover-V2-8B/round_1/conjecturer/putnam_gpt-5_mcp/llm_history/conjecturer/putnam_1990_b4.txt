Let \(\Gamma\) be the directed Cayley graph of \(G\) with generators \(a,b\):

- vertices: elements of \(G\),
- directed edges: \(x \to xa\) and \(x \to xb\).

Then:

1. \(\Gamma\) has \(n=|G|\) vertices and exactly \(2n\) directed edges.
2. Because \(a,b\) generate \(G\), \(\Gamma\) is strongly connected.
3. Every vertex has outdegree \(2\), and also indegree \(2\) (from \(xa^{-1}\) and \(xb^{-1}\)).

So \(\Gamma\) is an Eulerian directed graph, hence it has a closed Euler circuit using each directed edge exactly once.

Now list the starting vertices of the successive edges in such an Euler circuit as
\[
g(0),g(1),\dots,g(2n-1).
\]
Then:

- each step satisfies \(g(i+1)=g(i)a\) or \(g(i+1)=g(i)b\),
- since the tour is closed, this also holds cyclically mod \(2n\),
- each group element \(x\) appears exactly twice among the \(g(i)\), because the Euler tour leaves \(x\) exactly once along each of its two outgoing edges.

Thus the required \(g\) always exists. Therefore the proposition is true.

A small computational check on examples like \(S_3\) and \(D_4\) also confirms this construction.
