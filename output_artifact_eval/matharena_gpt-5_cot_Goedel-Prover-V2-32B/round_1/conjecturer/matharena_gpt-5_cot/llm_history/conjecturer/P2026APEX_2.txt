The key combinatorial fact for a simple orthogonal lattice polygon is that, when you traverse the boundary and count boundary “runs” along interior-side cells versus exterior-side cells, the excess is exactly the turning-number excess \(4\). Converting that run count into cell-types gives a uniform inequality of the form
\[
a(E,1)+a(E,2)\ge a(E,3)+4,
\]
hence in particular
\[
a(E,1)+a(E,2) > 1\cdot a(E,3)
\]
for every simple chessboard polygon.

So every \(k\le 1\) works; in particular \(k=1\) is in the set.

To see that nothing larger can be greatest, consider the family
\[
P_n=\{(x,0):0\le x\le 2n\}\cup\{(2i,1):0\le i\le n\}\cup\{(2i+1,-1):0\le i<n\},
\]
an alternating “comb” polyomino. For this family one computes
\[
a_3=4n,\qquad a_1+a_2=4n+8,
\]
so
\[
\frac{a_1+a_2}{a_3}=1+\frac{2}{n}\to 1.
\]
Thus no \(k>1\) can work for all polygons.

Therefore the greatest real number is \(1\).
