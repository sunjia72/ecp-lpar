Let  
\[
R_k(a)=\frac{e_k(a)}{e_1(a)^k},
\]
where \(e_j(a)\) is the \(j\)-th elementary symmetric sum of the positive multiset \(a\), and let \(n=\operatorname{card}(a)\ge k\).

By Maclaurin’s inequality for positive reals,
\[
\left(\frac{e_k(a)}{\binom{n}{k}}\right)^{1/k}\le \frac{e_1(a)}{n}.
\]
Raising to the \(k\)-th power gives
\[
e_k(a)\le \binom{n}{k}\frac{e_1(a)^k}{n^k},
\]
so
\[
\frac{e_k(a)}{e_1(a)^k}\le \frac{\binom{n}{k}}{n^k}
= \frac1{k!}\prod_{j=0}^{k-1}\left(1-\frac{j}{n}\right)\le \frac1{k!}.
\]
Thus \(1/k!\) is an upper bound.

To see it is best possible, take the multiset of \(n\) copies of \(1\). Then
\[
e_1=n,\qquad e_k=\binom{n}{k},
\]
hence
\[
\frac{e_k}{e_1^k}=\frac{\binom{n}{k}}{n^k}.
\]
As \(n\to\infty\),
\[
\frac{\binom{n}{k}}{n^k}\to \frac1{k!}.
\]
So for every \(M<1/k!\), some large enough \(n\) gives

\frac{\binom{n}{k}}{n^k}>M.

Therefore the sharp value is
