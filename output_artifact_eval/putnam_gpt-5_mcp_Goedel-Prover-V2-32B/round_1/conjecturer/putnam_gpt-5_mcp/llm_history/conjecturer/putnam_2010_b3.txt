- **necessary and sufficient:** \(n \ge 1005\).

### Why \(n \ge 1005\) is necessary
A position is **dead** if every pile \(i\) has fewer than \(i\) stones, since then no move is possible.

For \(m=2010\), the maximum total number of stones in a dead position is
\[
0+1+2+\cdots+2009=\frac{2010\cdot 2009}{2}=2010\cdot 1004.5.
\]
So for any \(n\le 1004\), the total \(2010n\) is at most this, and one can choose a dead configuration with total \(2010n\). That dead configuration is not the uniform one, so it cannot be transformed to all \(n\)'s. Hence such \(n\) are not in the set.

### Why \(n \ge 1005\) is sufficient
Now assume \(n\ge 1005\), so \(2010\le 2n\).

From any starting configuration of total \(2010n\):

1. First get at least one stone into pile \(1\).  
   Since total \(2010n > 0+1+\cdots+2009\), the position cannot be dead, so some move exists; eventually pile \(1\) can receive stones.

2. For every pile \(i>n\), reduce it to at most \(n\):  
   - if it has at least \(i\) stones, send \(i\) from pile \(i\) to pile \(1\);
   - if it has between \(n+1\) and \(i-1\) stones, send \(1\) from pile \(1\) to pile \(i\), then send \(i\) back from pile \(i\) to pile \(1\).  
     Net effect: pile \(i\) drops by \(i-1\), and pile \(1\) gains \(i-1\).
   
   Because \(i\le 2010\le 2n\), this process can always push every pile \(i>n\) down to \(\le n\).

3. Now all piles \(2,\dots,2010\) are \(\le n\). Use pile \(1\) to send single stones to any pile below \(n\) until all piles \(2,\dots,2010\) equal \(n\). Then pile \(1\) also has \(n\), since the total remains \(2010n\).

So every initial configuration can be transformed to the uniform one exactly when \(n\ge 1005\).
