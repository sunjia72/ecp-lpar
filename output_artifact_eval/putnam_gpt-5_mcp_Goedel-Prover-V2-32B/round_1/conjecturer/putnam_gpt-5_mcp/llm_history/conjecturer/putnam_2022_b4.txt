=== CONJECTURER ROUND ===
Let  
\[
d_i=x_{i+1}-x_i \qquad (i \bmod n).
\]
Then \(x_n=x_0\) means \(\sum_{i=0}^{n-1} d_i=0\).

For three consecutive terms \(x_i,x_{i+1},x_{i+2}\) to form an arithmetic progression as a set, one of them must be the average of the other two. In terms of \(d_i,d_{i+1}\), this gives exactly
\[
d_{i+1}=d_i,\qquad d_{i+1}=-2d_i,\qquad d_{i+1}=-\frac12 d_i.
\]
So every ratio \(d_{i+1}/d_i\) is in \(\{1,-2,-\tfrac12\}\).

Now normalize: divide by \(d_0\), and multiply by a large power of \(2\), so we get integers \(e_i\) of the form \(\pm 2^k\), hence none is divisible by \(3\). The same three relations become
\[
e_{i+1}=e_i,\quad e_{i+1}=-2e_i,\quad 2e_{i+1}=-e_i,
\]
and each implies \(e_{i+1}\equiv e_i \pmod 3\). Thus all \(e_i\) are congruent mod \(3\). Since \(\sum e_i=0\),
\[
0\equiv \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} e_i \equiv n e_0 \pmod 3,
\]
so \(3\mid n\).

A Python search over all possible ratio-patterns confirms there is no solution for \(n=4,5,6,7,8\), and the first solution is \(n=9\).

For every \(n=3m\ge 9\) (\(m\ge 3\)), we can construct one. Take cyclic differences
\[
\underbrace{1,\dots,1}_{2m-3},\ -\tfrac12,\ 1,\ \underbrace{-2,\dots,-2}_{m-1},\ 1,\ -\tfrac12.
\]
Their sum is
\[
(2m-3)-\tfrac12+1-2(m-1)+1-\tfrac12=0,
\]
so they define a cyclic sequence. Also each consecutive ratio is \(1,-2\), or \(-\tfrac12\), so every consecutive triple is an arithmetic progression. The partial sums are
\[
0,1,2,\dots,2m-3,\ 2m-\tfrac72,\ 2m-\tfrac52,\ 2m-\tfrac92,\dots,-\tfrac12,\tfrac12,
\]
which are all distinct.

So the valid \(n\) are exactly the multiples of \(3\) starting at \(9\).
