The intended statement is **true**.

Reasoning: this is the classical planar red-blue matching theorem.

Let \(n=|R|=|B|\). We prove by induction on \(n\).

- **Base case \(n=1\):** trivial.
- **Inductive step:** pick any vertex \(p\) of the convex hull of \(R\cup B\).  
  Suppose \(p\) is red (the blue case is symmetric). Since \(p\) is a hull vertex, all other points lie in some open half-plane through \(p\), so we can list them in angular order as
  \[
  q_1,q_2,\dots,q_{2n-1}.
  \]
  Define
  \[
  s_k = (\#\text{ blue among } q_1,\dots,q_k)-(\#\text{ red among } q_1,\dots,q_k).
  \]
  Then \(s_0=0\), and since among the remaining points there are \(n\) blue and \(n-1\) red points, \(s_{2n-1}=1\).

  Let \(k\) be the first index with \(s_k=1\). Then \(q_k\) must be blue, and \(s_{k-1}=0\). Hence among \(q_1,\dots,q_{k-1}\) there are equally many red and blue points. The same is then true among \(q_{k+1},\dots,q_{2n-1}\), because after removing \(p\) and \(q_k\) there remain \(n-1\) red and \(n-1\) blue points total.

  So the line \(pq_k\) splits the other points into two parts, each with equal numbers of red and blue points. By the induction hypothesis, each side admits a nonintersecting red-blue matching. Since each side lies in a different open half-plane bounded by the line \(pq_k\), those segments do not intersect each other or the segment \(pq_k\). Adding \(pq_k\) gives the desired matching for all points.

Thus the property in the theorem holds for all such \(R,B\), so the answer-proposition is `True`.
