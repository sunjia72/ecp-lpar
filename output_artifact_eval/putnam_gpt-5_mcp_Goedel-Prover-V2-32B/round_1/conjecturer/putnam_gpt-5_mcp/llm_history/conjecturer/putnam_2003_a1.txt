For any admissible sequence, let its positive part have length \(k\). Since the entries are positive and nondecreasing, and
\[
a(k-1)\le a(0)+1,
\]
every one of the first \(k\) terms must be either \(a(0)\) or \(a(0)+1\). Because the sequence is nondecreasing, it has the form
\[
\underbrace{m,\dots,m}_{k-r\text{ times}},\underbrace{m+1,\dots,m+1}_{r\text{ times}}
\]
for some \(m\ge 1\) and \(0\le r<k\).

Its sum is therefore
\[
n = km + r.
\]
For a fixed \(k\), this is exactly the Euclidean division of \(n\) by \(k\), so \(m=\lfloor n/k\rfloor\) and \(r=n\bmod k\), hence there is **at most one** such sequence for each \(k\). Also \(k\le n\), since the sum of \(k\) positive integers is at least \(k\). Conversely, for every \(k=1,2,\dots,n\), Euclidean division gives such a valid sequence. So there is exactly one sequence for each \(k\in\{1,\dots,n\}\), hence exactly \(n\) sequences.
