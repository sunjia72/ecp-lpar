\[
G(u,v)=\int_0^u\int_0^v f(x,y)\,dy\,dx .
\]
Then \(G(0,v)=G(u,0)=0\).

For any \(x,y\ge 0\) with \(x+y\le 1\), the square \([0,x]\times[y,y+x]\) is one of the allowed maximal squares, so its integral is \(0\). Hence
\[
0=\int_0^x\int_y^{y+x} f
 = G(x,y+x)-G(x,y),
\]
so
\[
G(x,y+x)=G(x,y). \tag{1}
\]

Likewise, the square \([x,x+y]\times[0,y]\) is also allowed, so
\[
0=\int_x^{x+y}\int_0^y f
 = G(x+y,y)-G(x,y),
\]
thus
\[
G(x+y,y)=G(x,y). \tag{2}
\]

Now if \(u+v\le 1\), then by using (1) or (2) backward:
- if \(u\ge v\), \(G(u,v)=G(u-v,v)\),
- if \(v\ge u\), \(G(u,v)=G(u,v-u)\).

So \(G\) is invariant under the Euclidean algorithm. If \(u/v\) is rational, repeated subtraction reaches an axis, where \(G=0\). Hence \(G=0\) for all rational points in the triangle \(u+v\le 1\), and by continuity for all points there.

Therefore \(G\equiv 0\) on \(u+v<1\), so its mixed partial derivative is zero there:
\[
f(u,v)=\frac{\partial^2 G}{\partial u\,\partial v}=0.
\]
Thus \(f=0\) on the lower triangle \(u+v<1\).

Apply the same argument to \(g(x,y)=f(1-x,1-y)\). The hypothesis is preserved by this reflection, so \(g=0\) on \(x+y<1\), i.e. \(f=0\) on \(x+y>1\).

By continuity, \(f=0\) also on \(x+y=1\). Hence \(f\equiv 0\) on the whole unit square.
