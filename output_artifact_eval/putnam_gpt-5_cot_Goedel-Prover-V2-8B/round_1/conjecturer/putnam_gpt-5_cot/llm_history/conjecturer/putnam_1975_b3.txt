Let
\[
R_k(a)=\frac{a.\mathrm{esymm}(k)}{(a.\mathrm{esymm}(1))^k}.
\]

For a multiset \(a=\{x_1,\dots,x_n\}\) of positive reals with \(n\ge k\):

- \(a.\mathrm{esymm}(1)=x_1+\cdots+x_n\).
- \(a.\mathrm{esymm}(k)=\sum_{1\le i_1<\cdots<i_k\le n} x_{i_1}\cdots x_{i_k}\).

Now expand \((x_1+\cdots+x_n)^k\).  
Each product of \(k\) **distinct** variables corresponding to one \(k\)-subset appears exactly \(k!\) times, and all other terms are also nonnegative. Hence
\[
(x_1+\cdots+x_n)^k \ge k!\, a.\mathrm{esymm}(k),
\]
so
\[
R_k(a)\le \frac1{k!}.
\]

So the best possible upper bound is at most \(1/k!\).

To see it is sharp, take the multiset of \(n\) copies of \(1\). Then
\[
R_k = \frac{\binom{n}{k}}{n^k}
= \frac1{k!}\prod_{j=0}^{k-1}\left(1-\frac{j}{n}\right),
\]
which tends to \(1/k!\) as \(n\to\infty\). Therefore for every \(M<1/k!\), some sufficiently large \(n\) gives \(R_k>M\).
